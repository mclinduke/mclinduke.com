Section 04

Brand Tone & Voice

How your copy reads and feels. This governs headlines, descriptions, CTAs, and all written content across both sites.

A

Quiet Confidence

6,500 miles. No shortcuts.
The path shaped the person.
I've walked every mile. From Georgia to Maine, Mexico to Canada, Kathmandu to 17,700 feet. The experience speaks for itself.
Never says: "EPIC!!", "Check out my awesome...", "Book NOW!", exclamation marks

Understated, let-the-work-speak. Short sentences, no hype. The confidence comes from restraint — you don't need to sell because the resume does it.

B

Bold & Direct

Built by the trail.
I've walked the miles.
Let me lead yours.
6,500+ miles across 10 major trails. Two complete thru-hikes. An evacuation at 17,700 feet. I know exactly what I'm doing out there.
Never says: "pretty good at...", "I think I can...", hedging language, passive voice

Confident, declarative, punchy. Not arrogant — just clear about what you bring to the table. First person, active voice, period at the end.

C

Storyteller

Every mile has a name.
Where the trail ends,
the story begins.
The sun hit the ridge at 14,000 feet and everything else fell away. Six months on the PCT will do that — strip you down to what matters and build you back up mile by mile.
Never says: generic adventure clichés, anything impersonal, corporate language

More narrative, evocative, cinematic. Draws people into the experience with vivid language. Makes them feel the trail before they've taken a step.

D

Premium Minimalist

6,500+
Duke.
Almost no body copy. Stats, images, and typography carry 90% of the weight. Maximum white space.

Extremely spare. One-word labels, single-line descriptions. Let the design and photography do the talking. The less said, the more exclusive it feels.
